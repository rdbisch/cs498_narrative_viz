An Analysis of Baseball Salary and Player Effectiveness

This is the essay explaining the structure and content of the narrative visualization.

These pages are part of rdb4@illinois.edu / rdbisch@gmail.com project for CS498 Data Visualization.

Messaging

The intent of my narrative visualization was to walk the reader through an analysis of professional baseball salaries.

Narrative Structure

The structure I choose to tell this story more closely resembles an interactive slide show. The textual content and charts are tied together with initial defaults that tie together--examples using Cubs, Whitesox; or Cubs, and the Angels, for example--but the user is encouraged to try the charts out and find supporting or counter evidence.

Visual Structure

I choose to use a few different elements to ensure continuity and understanding from the reader. The first, all but the last chart uses the same x-axis, mainly the year of play. Second, whenever there are options to compare team's side-by-side, I used consistent colors to show the left-selection vs. the right-selection. Third, transitions are used where it made sense to help the user understand what exactly was changing when different selections were made.

Scenes

There are four main scenes:

  • The Consumer Product Index (CPI) vs. Aggregate Baseball Salaries.

    This secenes sets the stage for the entire visualization by establishing how much faster than inflation these sports players salaries are. Currently it is a more or less a static display, through I suppose it could also by implemented to be consistent with the visualizaiton schemes below...

  • Player performance (per salary) over time.

    This is used to establish evidence that player's performance is not rising consummate with their income. TODO add annotation for this--maybe trendline?

  • Best-Player performance (per salary) over time.

    This is used to test the conjencture that averaging a team's washed out any signal. Spoiler: it doesn't.

  • Finally, Performance by Tenure.

    This is used to highlight the effect of salary controls on rookie players and to explain some abnormalities seen in the prior scene

The order displayed is very intentional. The first scene (CPI) sets the stage for the remainder--salaries are absurd. The second scene dives into a natural place to start looking into "why?". There are many other places we could have started--team revenue, ticket sales, ad revenue, merchandise sales, etc. But baseball affecienados only care about one thing. Performance! Having seen a contrary trend, the next two scenes further dive into two conjenctures the reader is likely to form themselves--why are teams seemingly getting worse? and why do rookies show up so well?
